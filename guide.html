A 2026 field guide · U.S. accounts

Where should the next dollar go?

Every tax-advantaged account does the same thing — it changes when the government taxes your money. Master that one idea and the whole landscape — 401(k)s, IRAs, HSAs, brokerage, savings — collapses into three buckets and one decision. This guide is the map: every account, the order to fill them, and which lever to pull at each income and each age.

01

The mental model

A 401(k) is not an investment. Neither is an IRA or an HSA. They are wrappers — legal containers that hold investments and change how those investments are taxed. There are only three kinds of wrapper.

Pre-tax Roth · tax-free Triple tax-free Taxable

Inside any wrapper you can hold the same S&P 500 index fund. What differs is the tax treatment at three moments: when money goes in, while it grows, and when it comes out. The three buckets at the top of this page are the entire taxonomy. Everything below is detail.

Three ideas do most of the work

Idea 1 — Tax-advantaged room is use-it-or-lose-it

Each January 1 you get a fresh annual allowance in each account. Don't fill it, and that room is gone permanently — you can't double up next year. A 35-year-old who skips one $7,500 IRA contribution doesn't lose $7,500; at a 7% real return over 30 years they lose roughly $57,000 of tax-free growth. The scarce resource isn't money, it's shelter.

Idea 2 — The whole game is one question

Is your marginal tax rate higher now or in retirement? If lower later → defer (pre-tax). If higher later → pay now (Roth). Since nobody can know future tax law or their own future income, the durable answer is tax diversification: build balances in more than one bucket so you have levers to pull whatever happens.

Idea 3 — Tax drag is the silent fee

In a taxable account, every dividend and every rebalance triggers a tax bill, and the money paid to taxes stops compounding. Over decades this "drag" quietly costs more than most fund fees. Sheltering removes it. That — not the upfront deduction — is the deepest reason to use these accounts.

One account breaks the pattern. The HSA is the only wrapper that can be tax-free at all three moments: deductible going in, untaxed while it grows, and tax-free coming out (for medical costs). Nothing else in the U.S. code does that, which is why it sits near the top of every funding order despite its small limit.

The accounts, one by one

Each card shows its tax bucket, who it's for, the 2026 limit, and the rule most likely to trip you up. Tags use the colors from the model above.

Workplace plan — 401(k) / 403(b) / 457(b) / TSP

Pre-taxor Roth

The backbone for most employees. 403(b) is the nonprofit/education version; 457(b) is for government and some nonprofits; TSP is the federal plan. Most modern plans let you choose pre-tax or Roth for each dollar.

2026 limit
$24,500 of your own salary deferral (per person, across all your plans). +$8,000 catch-up at age 50+; +$11,250 "super catch-up" at ages 60–63.
Match
Employer matching is free money and is the single highest-return move available. Always capture the full match first.
The 457(b) perk
Governmental 457(b)s have no 10% early-withdrawal penalty after you leave the job — useful for early retirement. A 457(b) limit is separate from a 401(k)/403(b), so some public employees can fund both.
Watch
Leaving a job? Roll old 401(k)s into your current plan or an IRA so you don't lose track — but see the backdoor-Roth trap in §9 before rolling into an IRA.

Traditional IRA

Pre-tax*

A personal account anyone with earned income can open. The deduction (the whole point) is income-limited once you're covered by a workplace plan.

2026 limit
$7,500 (shared with Roth IRA), +$1,100 at 50+. So $8,600 combined if 50+.
Deduction
Fully deductible if neither you nor your spouse has a workplace plan. If you're covered at work, the deduction phases out (see §3). High earners get no deduction — which is what makes the backdoor Roth (§9) useful.
Watch
Any pre-tax balance here pollutes the backdoor Roth via the pro-rata rule. Keep it at $0 if you plan to use the backdoor.

Roth IRA

Tax-free

The most flexible long-term account in the code. After-tax in, tax-free out, no required withdrawals ever for the original owner.

2026 limit
$7,500 (shared with Traditional IRA), +$1,100 at 50+.
Income limit
Direct contributions phase out at MAGI $153k–$168k (single) / $242k–$252k (married). Above that, use the backdoor (§9) — there is no income limit on that route.
Flexibility
Your contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn anytime, tax- and penalty-free — so a Roth can quietly double as a backstop emergency fund.
No RMDs
Never forced to withdraw. Ideal money to leave growing, or to leave to heirs.

HSA — Health Savings Account

Triple tax-free

The most tax-efficient account that exists, available only if you're on an HSA-eligible high-deductible health plan (HDHP). Treat it as a stealth retirement account, not a spending account.

2026 limit
$4,400 self-only / $8,750 family, +$1,000 at 55+. HDHP must have a deductible ≥ $1,700 (self) / $3,400 (family).
Triple play
Deductible going in (and, via payroll, also dodges Social Security + Medicare tax), grows untaxed, and comes out tax-free for medical costs — at any age.
The pro move
Pay medical bills out of pocket now, save the receipts, invest the HSA, and reimburse yourself tax-free decades later. After 65 it also covers Medicare premiums; non-medical withdrawals after 65 are just taxed like a Traditional IRA.
Watch
Contributions must stop once you enroll in Medicare. Plan the timing.

Solo 401(k)

Pre-taxor Roth

For the self-employed with no employees (a consultant, freelancer, or single-owner business). The most powerful shelter available to a one-person business.

2026 limit
Wear two hats: $24,500 as "employee" + up to 25% of net self-employment income as "employer," capped at $72,000 total (plus the age-50 and 60–63 catch-ups on the employee side).
Why it wins
It lets a high-earning solo operator shelter far more than a regular employee, and many providers support a Roth option and mega-backdoor after-tax contributions.
Watch
The "employee" $24,500 is shared with any W-2 401(k) you also have. The 25% employer piece is per-business.

SEP-IRA

Pre-tax

A simpler self-employed plan. Easier paperwork than a Solo 401(k), but less flexible.

2026 limit
Up to 25% of compensation, max $72,000. No employee-deferral piece and no catch-up.
vs Solo 401(k)
At lower self-employment income a Solo 401(k) usually shelters more (because of the flat $24,500 employee piece). A SEP-IRA balance also blocks the backdoor Roth via pro-rata. For most solo earners, the Solo 401(k) is the better default.

SIMPLE IRA

Pre-tax

A starter plan for small employers (≤100 employees). You'll meet it as an employee at a small company.

2026 limit
$17,000 (or $18,100 at some employers), +$4,000 at 50+; +$5,250 at 60–63. Lower than a 401(k) but with a required employer contribution.

Taxable brokerage

Taxable

A plain investment account with no contribution limit, no income limit, and no withdrawal rules. The home for everything after the sheltered buckets are full — and for any goal before age 59½.

Limit
None. Invest as much as you like, take it out anytime.
Tax
Dividends and interest are taxed yearly. Gains on assets held >1 year get the long-term capital-gains rate (0/15/20%), which is usually well below ordinary rates. Held <1 year, gains are taxed as ordinary income.
Edge
Inherited assets get a "step-up" in basis, and you can harvest losses (§9). With index funds it can be remarkably tax-efficient.

Cash — HYSA, money market, CDs, T-bills, I-bonds

Taxable

Not an investment for growth — a tool for safety and liquidity. Covered in depth in §8.

Role
Emergency fund (3–6 months of expenses) and any money you'll need within ~2–3 years.
2026 yields
Top high-yield savings ~4.0–4.4%; Treasury bills track the Fed (~3.5–3.75%); CDs up to ~4.3%.
Tax
Interest is taxed as ordinary income. T-bills and Treasury money-market funds are exempt from state income tax — a real edge for high earners in taxed states.

529 education plan

Tax-free (school)

A Roth-like wrapper for education. Relevant once you have kids or a known education goal.

Limit
No federal annual cap, but contributions are gifts: $19,000/yr per giver is the gift-tax-free amount (or front-load 5 years — ~$95k — at once).
Tax
Grows untaxed; tax-free for tuition and qualified costs. Many states add a deduction for residents.
2024+ perk
Up to $35,000 of an old 529 can be rolled into the beneficiary's Roth IRA (account must be 15+ years old; other limits apply) — so leftover funds aren't trapped.

New for 2026 — the Roth catch-up rule

If you earned more than $150,000 in wages from your employer in the prior year, any age-50+ catch-up contribution must now go in as Roth (after-tax), not pre-tax. If your plan offers no Roth option, you simply can't make the catch-up. High earners over 50 should confirm their plan supports Roth catch-up.

04

The funding waterfall

When a dollar is free to invest, this is the order that maximizes its after-tax value for most people. Work top to bottom; only move down once the rung above is full or doesn't apply.

  1. Starter emergency fund. One month of expenses in cash before anything else, so a flat tire doesn't become credit-card debt.
  2. 401(k) up to the full employer match. A 50% or 100% match is an instant, risk-free 50–100% return. Nothing else competes. Capture every matched dollar.
  3. Kill high-interest debt. Anything above ~7–8% (credit cards, some personal loans) is a guaranteed "return" by paying it off that beats the market on a risk-adjusted basis.
  4. Max the HSA (if you're on an HDHP). The only triple-tax-free account — punches far above its small limit.
  5. Max an IRA. Roth if your income qualifies; otherwise the backdoor Roth (§9). $7,500 of broad, low-cost, tax-advantaged growth.
  6. Finish the 401(k) to the full $24,500. Choose pre-tax vs Roth using §10.
  7. Full emergency fund — top up cash to 3–6 months of expenses (more if your income is variable or you're self-employed).
  8. Mega-backdoor Roth (if your plan allows after-tax contributions + conversions). Can add tens of thousands of Roth dollars a year. See §9.
  9. Other goals & taxable investing. 529 for kids, a house down payment, or a taxable brokerage for everything beyond the sheltered limits — invested with asset location (§7) and harvested for losses (§9).
How to bend the order

The waterfall is a default, not a law. Saving for a house in 3 years? That money belongs in cash (step 7-ish), not the market. Have a governmental 457(b)? It slots alongside the 401(k) and is especially good for early retirement. Expecting a much lower-income year ahead? Lean Roth now and pre-tax then. The logic underneath — capture free money, kill expensive debt, prefer tax-free growth, keep flexibility — is what to preserve.

05

Strategy by income

You don't need to follow all of this — find the band that matches your pay and do its list from the top down. Income controls just two things: which tax bucket is the better deal (the more you earn, the more you lean pre-tax), and which doors are open (a few close as you climb — but there's always a legal workaround).

Match — free money your employer adds when you put money into your 401(k). Always grab it first.
Bracket / rate — the percentage of tax you pay on your next dollar earned. It rises as you earn more.
Phase-out / "the door closes" — above a certain income, the rules no longer let you make a move directly.
Backdoor — a legal two-step that lets high earners get money into a Roth even when they're "too rich" to do it the normal way.

Self-employment overlay — applies at any income

The moment you have 1099 or business income, a Solo 401(k) opens up — letting you shelter up to $72,000 a year, far more than an employee's $24,500. You contribute both as the "employee" ($24,500) and as the "employer" (up to 25% of your net profit). It's the biggest single lever a consultant or business owner has, and it stacks on top of any regular-job 401(k) match. Two things to remember: the $24,500 employee piece is shared with any W-2 401(k) you also have, and a SEP or SIMPLE IRA balance can block your backdoor Roth (§9) — a Solo 401(k) sidesteps that.

06

Strategy by age

Income sets the bucket; age sets the tactics — how aggressive the investments are, which catch-ups unlock, and when the rare planning windows open.

20s — time is the whole advantage

  • Decades of compounding make this the best era for Roth: low bracket now, tax-free growth for 40+ years. A dollar at 25 is worth many times a dollar at 45.
  • Grab the match, build a starter emergency fund, and hold ~90–100% stocks — you have time to ride out every crash.
  • The amount matters less than starting. Even $100/month, begun now, becomes serious money.

30s — earnings rise, complexity arrives

  • Keep maxing what you can; begin shifting some contributions to pre-tax as your bracket climbs.
  • Use the HSA as a stealth retirement account. Marriage and kids bring a new filing status, a possible spousal IRA, and 529s.
  • Still mostly stocks — retirement is decades away.

40s — peak saving, peak pre-tax value

  • Often your highest-earning, highest-bracket years, so the pre-tax deduction is worth the most. Max everything; run backdoor and mega-backdoor Roth if you're a high earner.
  • Begin a gentle glide toward a few bonds, but stay stock-heavy — you still have 20+ years.
  • Guard against lifestyle creep: raises should mostly raise your savings rate.

50s — catch-ups unlock

  • At 50 you can add +$8,000 to the 401(k) and +$1,100 to an IRA every year. Use them.
  • Map your actual retirement income now; start de-risking modestly toward your target mix.
  • If you're a high earner over 50, remember the new rule: your catch-up may be required to be Roth (§3).

60s — the super catch-up and the conversion window

  • At ages 60–63 the 401(k) catch-up jumps to +$11,250 — a brief, larger window to top up.
  • The golden window: after you stop working but before Social Security and Required Minimum Distributions begin, your taxable income is often unusually low. These are the prime years to convert pre-tax money to Roth at low rates and to realize gains in the 0% bracket (§9, §11).
  • HSA contributions must end when you enroll in Medicare, but you can keep spending the balance tax-free.

70s and beyond — RMDs and giving

  • Required Minimum Distributions begin at age 73 (rising to 75 in 2033): the IRS forces taxable withdrawals from pre-tax accounts. Plan ahead so they don't spike your bracket or your Medicare premiums.
  • From age 70½ you can make Qualified Charitable Distributions — send IRA money straight to charity (up to $111,000/yr in 2026), satisfying the RMD with zero tax.
  • Roth accounts have no RMDs — leave them growing, or pass them to heirs as a tax-free inheritance.

Stocks, bonds & what goes inside

The accounts are wrappers; the investments inside them are the engine. Choosing a 401(k) does nothing until you pick what it holds — a surprising number of accounts sit in cash by default.

Bonds (investment-grade)~4–5%~1–2%Ballast — steadier, cushions stock crashes

Stocks vs. a high-yield savings account

This is the comparison people get stuck on. A HYSA paying ~4% feels great after years of near-zero rates — but after tax and ~2.7% inflation, its real return is close to zero. Stocks have a far higher expected return, but can fall 30–50% in a bad year. The deciding factor isn't which is "better" — it's your time horizon:

  • Need it within ~2–3 years (emergency fund, near-term house, a wedding) → cash. The market's short-term swings are too large to risk.
  • Won't touch it for 10+ years (retirement) → stocks. Historically, every 20-year holding period for a broad U.S. index has ended positive in real terms — though the past never guarantees the future.
  • In between → a blend, sliding toward cash/bonds as the date nears.

Keep the investments boring

  • Low-cost index funds (total U.S. market, total international, total bond) capture the market's return for a tiny fee. Fees compound against you exactly like tax drag — a 1% annual fee can quietly eat ~25% of a lifetime's growth.
  • Target-date funds bundle a diversified, automatically-de-risking mix into one fund matched to your retirement year — a fine all-in-one default, especially inside a 401(k).
  • Diversification and low cost beat stock-picking for almost everyone, almost always.
Asset location — the free lunch most people miss

Once you hold investments across all three buckets, which bucket holds which asset changes your tax bill with zero change in risk:

  • Put tax-inefficient assets (bonds, REITs — they spit out taxable income) in tax-deferred accounts, where that income is shielded.
  • Put tax-efficient assets (broad stock index funds — low dividends, gains you control) in the taxable account.
  • Put your highest-growth assets in the Roth, where decades of growth come out entirely tax-free.
08

Cash & HYSA, in depth

Cash has exactly two jobs: keep you safe and keep you liquid. It is not where wealth is built — holding too much is its own quiet mistake.

What cash is for

  • Emergency fund: 3–6 months of essential expenses (6–12 if your income is variable or self-employed). This is insurance, not an investment — its job is to exist, not to grow.
  • Short-term goals: anything you'll spend within ~2–3 years.
  • Dry powder: a modest reserve so you never have to sell investments at a bad moment.

The national-average savings rate is about 0.38%, and the largest banks often pay 0.01%. Leaving an emergency fund in a big-bank checking account quietly forfeits ~4% a year — easy money for a 10-minute transfer to an online HYSA.

High earner, taxed state → use Treasuries for cash

Interest from a HYSA or CD is taxed as ordinary income at both the federal and state level. T-bills and Treasury money-market funds are exempt from state income tax. In a high-tax state, a slightly-lower-headline Treasury yield often beats a HYSA after tax. (In a no- or low-income-tax state, the difference largely disappears — a HYSA's convenience may win.)

The cash-drag mistake

Beyond your emergency fund and near-term goals, large idle cash balances slowly lose purchasing power: ~4% interest minus tax minus ~2.7% inflation leaves almost nothing real. Cash earmarked for a goal a decade away is money quietly shrinking. Decide what each pile of cash is for — and invest anything with a long horizon.

09

Advanced tax moves

Once the basics are humming, these are the levers that move real money — most relevant to high earners and the self-employed.

Backdoor Roth IRA

If your income is over the Roth limit, you can't contribute directly — but there's no income limit on converting. So you contribute (non-deductible) to a Traditional IRA, then convert it to Roth shortly after. The result is the same as a Roth contribution. Both spouses can do it.

The pro-rata trap — read before rolling a 401(k) into an IRA

The IRS treats all your non-Roth IRAs as one pool when you convert. If you hold any pre-tax IRA money — including a rollover IRA from an old 401(k), or a SEP/SIMPLE IRA — your backdoor conversion is taxed proportionally. You can't convert only the after-tax dollars.

The fix: before doing a backdoor Roth, move that pre-tax IRA balance into a 401(k) or Solo 401(k) that accepts roll-ins (401(k)s aren't counted in the pro-rata math), emptying your IRA to $0 by December 31 of the conversion year. Clear the rollover IRA first, then run the backdoor cleanly.

Mega-backdoor Roth

A way to push tens of thousands more into Roth each year — if your 401(k) allows (a) after-tax (non-Roth) contributions and (b) in-plan Roth conversions or in-service withdrawals. You fill the gap between your $24,500 deferral + employer match and the $72,000 total limit with after-tax dollars, then immediately convert them to Roth. Confirm both features with your plan administrator; many plans support one but not the other.

Field manual · do it for real

The mega-backdoor Roth on Fidelity, step by step

Most large-company 401(k)s live at Fidelity NetBenefits, so here's the actual click-path. The whole setup is about 20 minutes, most of it one phone call — and once it's on, it runs itself every paycheck.

1

Confirm your plan has both switches

Your plan must allow after-tax contributions (a third bucket, separate from pre-tax and Roth) and a way to convert them — either in-plan Roth conversion or in-service withdrawal to a Roth IRA. Not all plans have both; some have neither.

NetBenefits → your 401(k) → Plan Information / Summary Plan Description — or just call the Fidelity workplace number on the site and ask both questions directly

If the answer is no to either, stop here — there's nothing to set up (but it's worth asking HR to add it; plans do).

2

Do the math for your after-tax amount

Your ceiling: $72,000 (2026) − your $24,500 deferral − everything your employer contributes. Example: max deferral + a $12,000 match leaves $35,500 of after-tax room. Divide by your remaining paychecks to get a per-paycheck percentage.

Make sure your regular pre-tax/Roth contribution is set to hit its own max first — after-tax dollars are the overflow, not a replacement.

3

Turn on after-tax contributions

NetBenefits → Contributions → Contribution Amount → set the "After-tax" percentage

It appears as its own line next to your pre-tax and Roth percentages. If you don't see an after-tax line at all, your plan doesn't offer it (see step 1).

4

Switch on automatic conversion — the step people miss

Without this, after-tax money just sits there growing taxable earnings, which defeats the point. You want Fidelity to convert every after-tax dollar to Roth the same day it lands.

Some plans: NetBenefits → your plan → a "Convert to Roth" link · Many plans: call the Fidelity workplace-plan phone line and say: "Please enable automatic Roth in-plan conversion of my after-tax contributions."

The rep flips a flag on your account. From then on, conversion is daily and automatic — no per-paycheck phone calls.

5

Verify after your next paycheck

NetBenefits → your plan → Balances → "Sources" — look for a "Roth In-Plan Conversion" line

If that source shows a balance matching your after-tax contribution, it's working. If the money is sitting in an "After-tax" source instead, the conversion flag isn't on — call back.

6

Know the tax paperwork (it's light)

Your after-tax contributions are never taxed again — only the sliver of earnings between contribution and conversion is taxable, and with same-day automatic conversion that's pennies. Each year Fidelity sends a Form 1099-R reporting the conversions; it flows into your return with little or no tax due. Nothing to file when you contribute.

Variations & caveats

Roth IRA instead of Roth 401(k): if your plan allows in-service withdrawals, you can periodically roll the after-tax money to a Roth IRA at Fidelity instead — more investment choice, but usually a manual request each time rather than automatic. Self-employed: Fidelity's off-the-shelf Solo 401(k) generally doesn't include after-tax contributions, so no mega-backdoor by default — third-party plan-document providers can add it; confirm current features with Fidelity before assuming. Every plan differs: menus move and rules vary by employer, so treat this as the map, and the rep on the phone as the ground truth.

Tax-loss & tax-gain harvesting

  • Loss harvesting: in a taxable account, sell an investment that's down to "bank" the loss, then buy a similar (not identical) fund to stay invested. Losses offset capital gains plus up to $3,000 of ordinary income a year, and unused losses carry forward forever. Avoid the wash-sale rule — don't rebuy the same security within 30 days.
  • Gain harvesting: in a year your taxable income sits in the 0% capital-gains bracket, deliberately sell winners and rebuy them — realizing gains at 0% and resetting your cost basis higher.

Roth conversion ladder

In low-income years — especially the gap between leaving work and starting RMDs/Social Security — convert chunks of pre-tax money to Roth, deliberately "filling up" the low brackets (e.g., converting just enough to reach the top of the 12% or 22% band). This pays tax now at a low rate, shrinks the future RMDs that would otherwise be taxed at a high rate, and builds tax-free Roth balances.

HSA as a stealth IRA

Covered in §2: pay medical costs out of pocket, invest the HSA, save receipts, and reimburse yourself tax-free years later. It's the only way to get money that was never taxed going in and is never taxed coming out.

Charitable leverage

  • Donate appreciated stock instead of cash: you skip the capital-gains tax and deduct the full value.
  • Donor-advised fund: "bunch" several years of giving into one high-income year for a big deduction, then grant to charities over time.
  • QCD at 70½: route RMD dollars straight to charity, tax-free — up to $111,000 in 2026 (§6).
State-tax timing — a relocation or income dip is an opportunity

A pre-tax deduction is worth your combined federal + state marginal rate. Moving from a high-tax state (e.g., California's top rate ~13.3%) to a low- or no-income-tax state (e.g., Arizona's flat 2.5%) sharply cuts the value of new pre-tax deductions — and makes Roth conversions much cheaper, since you'll pay little or no state tax on the converted amount. If a move or a low-income transition year is on the horizon, that's often the ideal time to convert pre-tax balances to Roth.

Traditional vs Roth

The recurring fork: deduct now and pay tax later, or pay now and never again? It comes down to comparing your marginal rate today against your expected rate in retirement.

Which way to lean
Favor Roth when…Favor pre-tax when…
You're young or in a low bracket now (10–22%)You're in a high bracket now (32–37%)
You expect higher tax rates (yours or the country's) laterYou expect a clearly lower bracket in retirement
You want no RMDs and maximum future flexibilityYou live in a high-tax state now (deduct at a high state rate)
You're a heavy saver — the after-tax limit shelters effectively moreYou'll retire to a low-tax state (defer state tax now, pay little later)
You're in a low- or no-income-tax state todayYou need the deduction to free up cash to invest at all
In practice: most high earners do both

The common high-income build is pre-tax 401(k) (deduct at 32–37% now) plus a backdoor Roth IRA and mega-backdoor Roth (tax-free later). That deliberately creates balances in two buckets, so in retirement you can blend withdrawals to control your bracket year by year. Tax diversification isn't indecision — it's the hedge against not knowing future tax law.

11

Spending it down

Accumulation gets the attention; the withdrawal phase quietly determines how much of it you keep. The same pot of money produces very different lifetime tax bills depending on the order you draw from each bucket.

The three pools, and the order to tap them

  • Conventional order: spend taxable first → then tax-deferred → leave Roth for last, so the tax-free account compounds the longest.
  • Usually better: blend across pools each year to "fill up" the low brackets — and use the low-income years before RMDs to run Roth conversions (§9). Draining only tax-deferred accounts late in life can trigger a painful RMD spike.

The 4% guideline

A rough rule of thumb: withdrawing about 4% of your portfolio in year one, then adjusting for inflation, has historically lasted ~30 years across most market histories. It's a planning heuristic, not a promise — it depends on your mix, fees, sequence of returns, and how flexible your spending can be. Use it to size a target, then adjust to real life.

Forced withdrawals and surcharges

  • RMDs at 73 (75 from 2033) force taxable distributions from pre-tax accounts whether you need the money or not — the reason to shrink those balances earlier via conversions. Roth accounts have none.
  • IRMAA: Medicare Part B/D premiums rise with income, on a ~2-year lookback. A big conversion or RMD can bump premiums — coordinate the timing.
  • Social Security timing: delaying benefits raises the monthly amount; claiming interacts with your other income and taxes. Worth modeling alongside your withdrawal plan.

Three worked profiles

The same framework, applied to three common situations. Find the one nearest yours and adapt.

A · Early-career employee

~26 years old · $70k salary · standard W-2 job

Bracket is low and the horizon is enormous — optimize for tax-free growth and the savings habit.

  • Contribute to the 401(k) up to the full match, as Roth if offered.
  • Open and max a Roth IRA ($7,500), invested in a low-cost total-market index or a target-date fund.
  • If on an HDHP, max the HSA and invest it. Build a 3–6 month cash emergency fund in a HYSA.
  • Hold ~90–100% stocks and largely ignore the headlines.

B · High-income W-2 household

~$300k combined · married · top of the 24–32% range

Over the Roth and Traditional-deduction limits — this is backdoor territory, with a focus on sheltering investment income.

  • Max both 401(k)s pre-tax (deduction is valuable here) and capture every match.
  • Backdoor Roth for each spouse, every year. Max the HSA.
  • Use the mega-backdoor Roth if either plan supports after-tax + conversions.
  • Invest the surplus in a taxable account with asset location (bonds in the 401(k), index funds taxable) and harvest losses annually. NIIT applies above $250k — another reason to shelter.
  • Open 529s for kids.

C · Going independent & relocating to a low-tax state

High earner launching a consultancy · moving from a high-tax to a low/no-tax state · marrying another high earner

A transition year is full of levers — new business plans unlock far more shelter, and changing income and state tax open rare conversion windows.

  • Open a Solo 401(k) for the consulting income — shelter up to $72,000 (employee $24,500 + 25% of net profit), far beyond an employee's limit. Compare against a SEP-IRA, but the Solo 401(k) usually wins and keeps the backdoor clean.
  • Clear any rollover/SEP IRA first. Before running the backdoor Roth, roll that pre-tax IRA into the Solo 401(k) (if it accepts roll-ins) to zero out the IRA and dodge the pro-rata trap (§9). Then do the backdoor cleanly — for both spouses.
  • Time Roth conversions. A lower-income transition year, especially after the move to a low-tax state, is the cheapest moment to convert pre-tax balances to Roth — you pay little or no state tax on the conversion.
  • Max the HSA if on an HDHP. Hold a larger cash buffer (6–12 months) since self-employment income is lumpy; favor T-bills/Treasury money-market for that cash.
  • Coordinate as a household once married: a new joint filing status shifts every bracket and phase-out, and a future cash-balance plan can shelter six figures if business income grows.
