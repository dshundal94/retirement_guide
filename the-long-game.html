Story one · the deep dive

The Physician

Meet Dr. Anaya Rao. She finishes medical school at 26 with a stack of federal loans and a resident's salary. In ten years she'll likely out-earn almost everyone — but the choices she makes while broke shape her next forty years more than anything she does rich.

CH 1

The setup — debt meets a resident's paycheck

Set the shape of Anaya's start. Median med-school debt runs around $200–250k; residency pays like a teacher while working like two jobs. Her federal loans go onto an income-driven repayment (IDR) plan — the payment tracks her income, not her balance. Notice what happens to the "required payment" as you move her salary: the debt can be enormous while the payment stays small. That gap is the whole physician story.

The quiet mechanicWhen the income-driven payment is smaller than the interest, the balance grows during residency. That sounds terrifying — but if she's headed for loan forgiveness, it's actually fine, and fighting it is the mistake. The next two chapters show why.
CH 2

The residency fork — Roth or Traditional?

Anaya can save a little even now — set how much below. Conventional wisdom says a low earner should choose Roth (pay the small tax now, never again). But residents aiming for Public Service Loan Forgiveness have a twist: Traditional (pre-tax) contributions lower her AGI, and her loan payment is a percentage of AGI. Every pre-tax dollar cuts her required loan payment by ten cents — and under PSLF, whatever she doesn't pay eventually gets forgiven. Flip the toggle and watch the chips.

The lessonThe "right" bucket isn't fixed — it depends on the loan strategy. Chasing forgiveness → Traditional (shrink AGI, shrink payments, maximize what's forgiven). Planning to refinance & pay off → Roth (her bracket is the lowest it will ever be). One toggle, opposite answers. This is the kind of interaction a generic "Roth when young" rule completely misses.
CH 3

The loan fork — forgiveness or the fast payoff

Now the biggest fork of all. Path A — PSLF: she works at a nonprofit hospital (most academic hospitals qualify), makes 120 income-driven payments (10 years, residency counts!), and the entire remaining balance is forgiven, tax-free. Path B — Refinance: she takes a private loan at a lower rate and attacks it hard as an attending. Toggle between them and watch the two loan curves — the fork chart below is the signature image of her whole story.

CH 4

The attending jump — and the creep

At last: the attending contract. Income triples overnight — and so does the danger. The classic physician-finance advice is "live like a resident" for the first few years: keep resident-level spending while earning attending money, and shovel the difference into the waterfall (max 401(k), backdoor Roth — she's far over the Roth income limit now — HSA, then taxable). Slide the "live like a resident" lever and watch what those few disciplined years are worth at 65.

The lessonThe jump years are a once-per-career anomaly: income has tripled but lifestyle hasn't caught up yet. Lifestyle creep is a one-way ratchet — spending is easy to raise and brutal to cut. Deciding the savings rate before the first attending paycheck lands is worth more than a decade of optimization later.

Sam Okafor lands a tech job at 23 — a salary most people wait twenty years for. Sam's enemies aren't scarcity; they're tax drag on a growing taxable pile, and a Roth door that closes fast at this income.

CH 2

The secret door — 403(b) and 457(b)

Here's the structural gift: most school districts offer both a 403(b) and a 457(b) — and their $24,500 limits are separate. A teacher can shelter up to $49,000 a year, double what a private-sector engineer gets. Better still, the governmental 457(b) has no early-withdrawal penalty after leaving the job — at any age. It's the cleanest early-retirement vehicle in the entire code, sitting in a staff-lounge brochure nobody reads.

The Founder

Leo Tran quits his job at 31 to consult solo. No match, no HR, no safety net — but also no ceiling. Self-employment trades security for the largest personal tax shelter in the code, if he knows to open it.

CH 1

The setup — lumpy money

Consulting income arrives in lumps — a great quarter, then crickets. Rule one is a bigger cash buffer than any employee needs: 6–12 months, parked in T-bills or a HYSA, before aggressive investing. Set his business profit below.

CH 2

The fork — the Solo 401(k) nobody tells you about

An employee gets $24,500 of 401(k) room. Leo, as his own employer, can open a Solo 401(k) and contribute twice: $24,500 as employee plus up to 25% of profit as employer, to a $72,000 cap. No form from HR — because he is HR. Toggle whether he ever opens one; the readouts show the shelter and the tax saved at his marginal rate, live, as you move the profit slider.

CH 3

The variance tax — and what smooths it

One more founder-only truth: bad years happen. The engine here models a rough patch — profit cut in half for two years in his 40s. Watch how little it dents the long run when the buffer and the shelter are in place. The system absorbs the shock; panic-selling in the dip is what turns a bad year into a bad decade.
