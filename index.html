Where should your next dollar go?

401(k), Roth, HSA, backdoor, mega-backdoor — the jargon makes this sound complicated. It isn't. Every one of these accounts does the same single thing: it changes when the government taxes your money. There are only three answers, and they fit in the table beside this. Learn that, and the rest is detail.

Read across the rows. Pre-tax pays tax later, Roth pays now and never again, taxable pays the whole way. Then look at the green row: the HSA is the only account in the U.S. tax code that escapes tax at all three moments — which is why it outranks nearly everything, despite its small limit.

What this is, and what it isn't

A free educational reference — the explanation most people never got. It is not financial or tax advice. Nobody here is your advisor or your CPA, and the right move always depends on your whole picture: income, goals, state, debts, and what lets you sleep at night.

All figures are for tax year 2026, checked against IRS sources in July 2026. Limits and brackets change every year, so verify the current numbers before you act. For the moves with real consequences — Roth conversions, the backdoor's pro-rata trap, self-employed plan setup — talk to a fee-only fiduciary advisor and a CPA who can see your actual return.

The simulations are deliberately simplified: one steady return, 2026 rules held constant, today's dollars. They exist to make decisions visible, not to predict your future.
